# Consumer Brand Naming Reference

Sensory, experiential, and strategic naming techniques for consumer, lifestyle, food, beauty, fashion, and DTC brands. Load this file only when the brief is for a consumer/physical product. Skip for B2B/enterprise naming.

### Key Rules
1. **Run the Five Senses Scan for every physical product** — ask what it looks/smells/feels/tastes/sounds like. This is the #1 technique for consumer naming.
2. **Invert expected sensory associations** (Sensory Dissonance) — but calibrate the gradient: light for most brands, medium for confident brands, skip for kids/enterprise
3. **Borrow naming codes from a more desirable adjacent category** — fragrance language for skincare, spirits for non-alc, fashion for supplements

**Cross-references:** For core naming categories → `naming-categories.md`. For riffing/compound/affix techniques → `naming-techniques.md`. For brand archetypes → `brand-psychology.md`.

## Sensory & Experiential Naming

Names that work through physical sensation, emotional feeling, and sensory association rather than conceptual meaning. This is the primary generation approach for consumer brands — products people touch, taste, smell, wear, or put on their skin. But it also applies to any brand where the EXPERIENCE matters more than the function.

### The Five Senses Scan (Generation Technique)

For any product, systematically generate names by asking what the product makes you EXPERIENCE through each sense. This produces candidates that conceptual thinking ("what does it DO?") never reaches.

| Sense | Question | Example (luxury soap) | Names generated |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Sight** | What does it look like? Color, texture, shape, light. | Creamy white, smooth, translucent, soft edges | Alabaster, Ivory, Opal, Glaze, Pearl |
| **Smell** | What does it smell like? Or what SHOULDN'T it smell like? | Cedar, lavender, rain, smoke, earth | Woodsmoke, Petrichor, Resin, Moss, Ember |
| **Touch** | What does it feel like? Temperature, texture, weight, pressure. | Silky, cool, slippery, smooth, melting | Velvet, Slick, Balm, Silk, Suede |
| **Taste** | What does it taste like? (Even for non-food products — the association matters.) | Honey, salt, bitter, cream, citrus | Brine, Nectar, Clove, Tonic, Zest |
| **Sound** | What does it sound like? The product in use, the environment, the mood. | Water running, quiet, a whisper, a crackle | Hush, Murmur, Ripple, Still, Lull |

**The technique:**
1. Run the Five Senses Scan for the product — generate 5-10 words per sense
2. Test each word as a standalone name candidate
3. Cross-pollinate: combine a word from one sense with a word from another ("Mossvelvet," "Emberlull," "Brinesilk")
4. The strongest sensory names are often single words from unexpected senses — a soap named after a sound (Hush), a candle named after a texture (Grit)

**When to use:** Consumer products (beauty, food, beverage, fashion, home, wellness), hospitality brands, lifestyle brands, any product where the user experience is primarily sensory rather than functional.

### Sensory Dissonance (The Cowshed Strategy)

The most distinctive consumer names often come from INVERTING the expected sensory association. The gap between what the name implies and what the product delivers creates curiosity, memorability, and brand story.

**The technique:**
1. **Map the expected sensory territory** for the product category:
   - Soap → clean, fresh, floral, gentle, pure, light
   - Skincare → clinical, gentle, natural, botanical, scientific
   - Energy drink → power, speed, electricity, extreme, bold
   - Luxury hotel → elegance, heritage, service, quiet, refined

2. **Deliberately invert each association:**
   - Clean → **Dirty**. Fresh → **Stale**. Floral → **Barnyard**. Gentle → **Rough**. Pure → **Crude**.
   - Clinical → **Drunk**. Gentle → **Savage**. Natural → **Toxic**. Botanical → **Chemical**.
   - Power → **Idle**. Speed → **Lazy**. Extreme → **Mild**. Bold → **Quiet**.

3. **Test whether the inversion creates interesting dissonance:**
   - Does the gap between name and product create curiosity? ("Why is a luxury soap called Cowshed?")
   - Does it create a story people retell? ("It's named after the converted cowshed where the spa was built")
   - Does it signal confidence? (A brand secure enough to name itself something "wrong" signals quality)
   - Or does it just confuse? (If the gap is TOO large or the negative association too strong, it fails)

**Real examples of sensory dissonance:**

| Name | Product | Expected territory | Inversion | Why it works |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Cowshed** | Luxury spa/soap | Fresh, floral, clean | Barnyard, manure, mud | The absurdity signals confidence. "We're so good we don't need a pretty name." |
| **Drunk Elephant** | Premium skincare | Clinical, pure, botanical | Intoxicated, animal, wild | Implies natural ingredients (elephants eat fermented marula fruit). The "drunk" breaks the sterile skincare convention. |
| **Dirty Lemon** | Wellness drinks | Clean, pure, healthy | Dirty, spoiled | Implies authenticity — not over-processed. The contradiction sticks in memory. |
| **Ugly Drinks** | Premium flavored water | Beautiful, aspirational | Ugly, rejected | Anti-beauty-standard positioning. Implies substance over surface. |
| **Lush** | Cosmetics | Fresh, clean, clinical | Overripe, excessive, drunk | "Lush" means both "luxuriant growth" and "a drunk." The double meaning adds texture without being negative. |
| **Method** | Cleaning products | Fresh, powerful, chemical | Scientific, neutral, procedural | Inverts the "powerful cleaner" trope with calm systematic energy. |
| **Death Wish** | Coffee | Comfort, warmth, morning | Death, danger, extreme | For the "strongest coffee" positioning, the inversion IS the product promise. |
| **Liquid Death** | Water | Pure, natural, refreshing | Death, metal, toxic | Punk energy for water. The maximum dissonance possible — death for the most life-giving substance. |

**The Dissonance Gradient — how far to push:**

| Level | What it means | When to use | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Light** | Unexpected category, same positive valence | Most consumer brands. Safe. | Haze (for clear wine), Murk (for quality wine), Kiln (for candles) |
| **Medium** | Inverted sensory association, bridgeable with a story | Brands with strong design/identity to bridge the gap | Cowshed (barnyard for luxury spa), Drunk Elephant (intoxication for skincare) |
| **Heavy** | Shock, confrontation, deliberate offense | Brands targeting subculture audiences who value subversion. Requires design excellence. | Liquid Death (death for water), Fucking Awesome (profanity for skatewear) |

**When to SKIP sensory dissonance entirely:**
- **Audiences under 10:** Children don't appreciate subversion. They process names literally. "Crud" to a 4-year-old means gross, not craft.
- **Enterprise B2B:** A Fortune 500 CISO won't approve "Barnyard Security." Conservative buyers expect convention.
- **Healthcare/clinical:** Patients need reassurance, not provocation.
- **When the brand has zero design budget:** Dissonance requires strong visual identity to bridge the name-product gap. Without it, the name just confuses.

**When sensory dissonance works best:**
- When the product ALREADY lives in dissonance (natural wine IS funky, fermented hot sauce IS "spoiled")
- When the brand has strong visual identity to bridge the gap
- When there's a genuine story behind the inversion
- When the target audience appreciates subversion (younger, culture-savvy consumers)
- When the category is saturated with "expected" names

**Generation process for sensory dissonance:**
1. Run the Five Senses Scan to map the EXPECTED sensory territory
2. For each expected association, write its opposite
3. Choose your gradient level (light/medium/heavy) based on audience and brand resources
4. Generate 10-15 names from the inverted territory at the chosen level
5. Filter: which inversions create CURIOSITY vs CONFUSION vs DISGUST?
6. For the curiosity-creators: can you construct a story that bridges the gap?

### Emotional Temperature Mapping

Beyond brand personality dimensions (Authority ↔ Approachability), map the BODILY FEELING a name creates. This is visceral, pre-cognitive, and often more honest than analytical evaluation.

**Temperature dimensions:**

| Dimension | Warm end | Cool end | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Thermal** | Warm, cozy, glowing | Cool, crisp, icy | Ember (warm) vs Frost (cool) |
| **Weight** | Heavy, grounded, substantial | Light, airy, floating | Anvil (heavy) vs Wisp (light) |
| **Energy** | Energized, kinetic, buzzing | Calm, still, quiet | Bolt (energy) vs Hush (calm) |
| **Texture** | Rough, gritty, raw | Smooth, polished, sleek | Grit (rough) vs Silk (smooth) |
| **Brightness** | Bright, vivid, saturated | Dark, muted, shadowed | Vivid (bright) vs Dusk (dark) |

**Using this in naming:**
1. During the brief, ask the user: "Where should this name land on warm↔cool, heavy↔light, energized↔calm?" These are more intuitive than abstract personality descriptors.
2. During generation, filter candidates by emotional temperature. A name that the user says should feel "warm, light, calm" eliminates hard-consonant, heavy-stress candidates immediately.
3. During shortlisting, check each finalist against the target temperature. A mismatch between intended and actual temperature is a positioning red flag.

**The body test:** Say the name aloud and notice your physical response. Does your jaw clench (tension, authority)? Does your face relax (warmth, comfort)? Do you lean forward (curiosity, energy)? Do you exhale (calm, trust)? The body responds before the mind evaluates — this is System 1 naming data.

### Synaesthetic Naming (Cross-Sensory)

Names that use a word from one sense to evoke a different sense. These are cognitively sticky because they activate multiple sensory pathways simultaneously — the brain processes them more deeply than single-sense words.

**Common synaesthetic patterns:**

| Word's primary sense | Used to evoke | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Touch → Sound** | "Velvet" voice, "Smooth" jazz | Velvet, Silk, Satin (for audio/music brands) |
| **Touch → Intelligence** | "Sharp" mind, "Rough" estimate | Sharp, Edge, Point (for analytics/tech) |
| **Visual → Flavor** | "Bright" taste, "Dark" chocolate | Bright, Vivid, Deep (for food/beverage) |
| **Visual → Sound** | "Loud" colors, "Muted" tones | Loud, Muted, Tone (for fashion/design) |
| **Temperature → Personality** | "Warm" person, "Cool" brand | Warm, Cool, Crisp (for lifestyle brands) |
| **Taste → Experience** | "Sweet" deal, "Bitter" truth | Sweet, Bitter, Tart, Zest (for experiences) |
| **Sound → Visual** | "Harmonious" design, "Dissonant" look | Harmony, Chord, Pitch (for design tools) |

**Generation technique:**
1. Identify the product's PRIMARY sense (skincare = touch, music = sound, food = taste)
2. Generate names from a DIFFERENT sense that describes the same quality
3. The cross-wiring creates depth: "Velvet" for a music brand communicates "this sounds luxurious" more evocatively than "Luxsound"

**Why this works:** Neuroimaging research shows that synaesthetic language activates broader cortical networks than literal language. A name like "Velvet" (touch-word used for sound) lights up both somatosensory and auditory cortex, creating a richer memory trace than a word that activates only one area. This is the neural basis for why multi-sensory names feel more "real" and memorable.

**Anti-pattern:** Don't force synaesthesia where it doesn't fit. "Velvet" for a tech infrastructure company would be confusing, not enriching. Synaesthetic naming works when the cross-sensory association genuinely captures something about the product experience.

## Consumer Brand Elicitation Techniques

Generation techniques specifically for consumer, lifestyle, food, beauty, fashion, and DTC brands. These work through EXPERIENCE and CONTEXT rather than conceptual meaning.

### Mood Board → Name
Start with visual inputs, derive naming direction from imagery. This inverts the usual flow (concept → name) to (aesthetic → name).

**The technique:**
1. Ask the user: "Show me 5 images that feel like your brand — products, places, textures, art, anything." Or generate a verbal mood board together: "Describe the visual world of this brand — colors, materials, environments, lighting."
2. List every concrete noun visible or implied in the mood board
3. Test each as a name candidate
4. The best mood-board names carry the brand's entire visual world in a single word

**Example:** A skincare brand's mood board shows: raw concrete, Japanese ceramics, morning fog, undyed linen, a single wildflower. Nouns mined: Concrete, Ceramic, Fog, Linen, Wildflower → **Kiln** (implied by ceramics), **Gauze** (implied by linen), **Haze** (implied by fog), **Stone** (implied by concrete).

### Color → Name Mapping
"What color IS this brand?" Colors generate vocabulary that carries emotional weight without describing the product.

**Color vocabulary by palette:**

| Palette | Words to mine | Brand energy |
|---|---|---|
| **Matte black + terracotta** | Soot, Char, Rust, Clay, Oxide, Ochre, Sienna | Raw, warm, craft |
| **Sage green + cream** | Moss, Lichen, Bone, Ivory, Chalk, Celadon | Natural, gentle, quiet |
| **Navy + gold** | Midnight, Ink, Gilt, Admiral, Harbor | Premium, institutional, heritage |
| **White + neon** | Blank, Flash, Pulse, Signal, Volt | Modern, clinical, bold |
| **Dusty pink + grey** | Blush, Ash, Haze, Petal, Slate, Dusk | Soft, editorial, feminine |
| **Forest + amber** | Canopy, Resin, Sap, Pine, Ember, Umber | Grounded, outdoors, warm |

**The technique:** Ask "what color palette IS this brand?" Mine the color vocabulary. Even if the final name isn't a color word, the palette shapes phonetic and emotional direction.

### Ingredient / Process Vocabulary Mining
Mine the actual MAKING process for name candidates. The maker's own vocabulary is a goldmine because it carries authenticity that conceptual naming can't fake.

**By product type:**

| Product | Process vocabulary | Strong name candidates |
|---|---|---|
| **Fermented foods** | culture, mother, starter, wild, lacto, bloom, brine, cure | Culture, Mother, Starter, Bloom, Brine |
| **Distilled spirits** | proof, still, spirit, barrel, char, wash, feints, cut | Proof, Still, Feints, Cut |
| **Baked goods** | leaven, crust, crumb, bloom (bread rising), score, proof (dough) | Leaven, Crumb, Score |
| **Textiles/fashion** | warp, weft, selvage, nap, hand, drape, bolt, loom | Weft, Nap, Bolt, Loom |
| **Ceramics** | raku, bisque, slip, glaze, fire, kiln, flux, shard | Raku, Bisque, Slip, Kiln |
| **Skincare/beauty** | emulsion, tincture, serum, balm, elixir, oil, essence | Balm, Tincture, Elixir |
| **Coffee** | bloom, extraction, crema, pull, tamp, grind, origin | Bloom, Crema, Origin |
| **Candles** | pour, throw (scent throw), pool, wick, cure, batch | Pour, Throw, Cure |

**The technique:** Ask the founder: "Walk me through how you make this product, step by step. What are the technical terms?" Every step has vocabulary. The name might be hiding in step 3.

### Origin Story / Founder Narrative Mining
Many iconic consumer brands come from personal stories, not branding exercises.

**Real examples:**
- **Patagonia** — the place Yvon Chouinard dreamed of climbing
- **Warby Parker** — two characters from Jack Kerouac novels
- **Glossier** — from the blog "Into The Gloss"
- **Allbirds** — merino wool from New Zealand (all birds → the source animal)
- **Aesop** — the fabulist who told stories with morals

**The technique:**
1. Ask: "Why did you start this? What's the personal story?"
2. Ask: "Is there a place, person, book, song, or moment that's central to why this exists?"
3. Mine the narrative for proper nouns, place names, character names, and evocative details
4. The strongest origin-story names carry the founder's passion without requiring the backstory to be told every time

**"No story?" fallback:** If the founder has no distinctive origin story (or the story doesn't contain evocative material), redirect to **Place Evocation** ("Where does this brand spiritually live?"), **Ritual Mapping** ("How does the customer use this?"), or **Cultural Tension** ("What argument is this brand entering?"). These techniques achieve the same emotional grounding through different entry points.

### Cultural Tension Naming
Name from the TENSION the brand enters, not the product it sells. The tension is the cultural conversation the brand participates in.

**Real examples:**

| Brand | Product | Cultural tension | How the name addresses it |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Liquid Death** | Water | "Water is boring" | Maximum dissonance — death for the most life-giving substance |
| **Oatly** | Oat milk | "Alt-milks try too hard to be milk" | Honest, self-deprecating, doesn't pretend to be dairy |
| **Ugly Drinks** | Flavored water | "Pretty packaging = artificial ingredients" | Rejects beauty standards → implies authenticity |
| **Who Gives A Crap** | Toilet paper | "Nobody wants to think about toilet paper" | Confronts the awkwardness head-on |
| **Death Wish** | Coffee | "Coffee should be stronger" | Names the extreme desire directly |

**The technique:**
1. Ask: "What's frustrating or broken about your category right now?"
2. Ask: "What does your brand believe that competitors would disagree with?"
3. The tension IS the naming territory. Mine it for words that express the brand's position in the argument.

### Ritual / Usage Moment Mapping
How does the customer USE this product? The ritual generates vocabulary that embeds the name in the user's daily life.

**The technique:**
1. Map the usage ritual: When (morning/evening/weekend)? Where (bathroom/kitchen/gym)? How (alone/shared)? What's the mood (energized/calm/indulgent)?
2. Mine each element for name candidates
3. "Ritual" is literally a vitamin brand. "Sunday Riley" evokes a lazy weekend. "Saturday Skin" evokes the weekend glow. "Drunk Elephant" evokes the nighttime skincare ritual. "Morning Brew" evokes... morning.

**Time-of-day vocabulary:** Dawn, Morning, Noon, Dusk, Twilight, Evening, Midnight, Sunday, Weekend, Daily

### Place / Environment Evocation
"Where does this brand LIVE? Not where it's sold — where is it spiritually from?"

**The technique:**
1. Ask the founder: "If I could teleport to the place that IS your brand, where would I be?"
2. Mine the vocabulary of that place — not the place name itself, but the THINGS in that place
3. A Japanese forest: Moss, Cedar, Still, Root, Bark. A Brooklyn rooftop: Tar, Ledge, Skyline, Grit. A Scandinavian kitchen: Pine, Grain, Linen, Kettle.

**Why this works:** Places carry complete sensory worlds. Naming from a place imports its entire atmosphere — light quality, temperature, sounds, smells, textures — in a single word.

### Social-Native Testing
For DTC brands, social media presence is a naming constraint, not an afterthought.

**Tests to run on every consumer finalist:**

| Test | Question | Red flag |
|---|---|---|
| **Hashtag test** | Does #[name] look clean? Is it already crowded? | #bloom has 30M posts (too generic). #cowshed is ownable. |
| **Handle test** | Is @[name] available or close variants? | @bloom is taken everywhere. @drinkmurk is ownable. |
| **Caption test** | "Just discovered @[name] and I'm obsessed" — does it read naturally? | Names with hard consonant clusters look awkward in casual text |
| **Story tag test** | Would someone tag this brand in an Instagram story? | Only if the name is short, memorable, and spellable after one exposure |
| **Verbal sharing test** | "You HAVE to try [name]" — does it survive word-of-mouth? | If the listener has to ask "how do you spell that?" the name has friction |

### The Shelf Test
"Imagine this name on a physical shelf between [competitor A] and [competitor B]. Which bottle/box/jar do you pick up?"

**The technique:**
1. List the 3-5 products that would be physically adjacent on a retail shelf
2. For each finalist name, visualize it on a label/package between those competitors
3. Ask: Which one creates the most CURIOSITY GAP — the gap between "what I expect this name to mean" and "what product it's actually on"?
4. Curiosity gaps drive pick-up. "Cowshed" between "Molton Brown" and "Jo Malone" creates maximum curiosity.

### The Gift Test
"Would you feel proud giving someone a product called [name]?"

Gift-ability drives a massive share of DTC revenue. A name that's embarrassing to gift vs one that's delightful to gift is a real commercial consideration.

**The test:**
1. Imagine wrapping this product as a birthday gift
2. The recipient reads the name on the box BEFORE opening it
3. What do they think? Curiosity? Delight? Confusion? Cringe?
4. Names that generate "Oh, what's THIS?" are gift-ready. Names that generate "...okay" are not.

**Names that gift well:** Cowshed (story-starter), Aesop (intellectual cachet), Le Labo (exclusive feel), Glossier (aspirational), Drunk Elephant (conversation piece)
**Names that gift awkwardly:** Crud (receiver might not get the joke), Ugly Drinks (the name IS the product's problem), Death Wish (depends heavily on recipient)

### French Naturalized Vocabulary Mining

Mine French words that have been fully naturalized into English — words English speakers already use and understand but that carry an elevated precision and sophistication. These words read as sophisticated English, not as "foreign borrowing." This distinction matters: the user who rejects "Cartouche" as pretentious may love "Cachet" because it feels English.

**Productive vocabulary by semantic domain:**

| Domain | Words | Brand energy |
|---|---|---|
| **Quality/Status** | cachet, prestige, calibre, finesse, élan, panache, aplomb, allure | Premium, assured, status-signaling |
| **Design/Craft** | motif, trait, contour, silhouette, relief, rapport, nuance, detail | Precise, considered, design-forward |
| **Energy/Character** | verve, pique, flair, coup, brio, savant, vogue | Dynamic, spirited, culturally fluent |
| **Form/Arrangement** | ensemble, tableau, montage, collage, genre, niche | Curated, editorial, collected |
| **Occasion/Entry** | debut, soirée, entrée, encore, premiere | Event-driven, momentous |

**When to use:** Consumer, fashion, lifestyle, premium/masstige brands — especially when the Price-Signal Calibration target is luxury or masstige (see Price-Signal Calibration below). Session testing showed French naturalized words had a consistently high hit rate when the user's taste ran toward "elevated but approachable."

**The calibration test:** Would an English speaker use this word in a normal sentence without feeling like they're "speaking French"? "That restaurant has a certain cachet" — yes. "That's a recurring motif" — yes. "She wore a beautiful cartouche" — no. If the word passes this test, it's naturalized enough to work as a brand name in English-speaking markets.

**Anti-pattern:** Avoid words that still feel "pointedly French" to an English speaker — je ne sais quoi, hors d'oeuvres, au courant. The technique works because the words feel like elevated English, not borrowed French. Also avoid the same word with a French accent mark if the unaccented version exists in English (détail vs detail) — the accent adds pretension without adding meaning.

**Real examples:** Vogue (magazine), Allure (magazine), Cachet (has been used in fashion), Montage (hotels), Tableau (software), Encore (events).

## Consumer Brand Strategy Techniques

Advanced naming techniques for consumer brands that go beyond generation into market positioning, cultural fluency, and commercial viability.

### Desired-Self Naming
Name from the identity the buyer wants to PROJECT, not the product they're buying. Consumer purchases are identity signals before they are utility decisions.

**The question:** "What kind of person buys this? What does owning this say about me?"

**Examples:** Supreme (I'm in the know), Glossier (I'm effortlessly beautiful), Patagonia (I care about the planet AND look good), Aesop (I have refined taste), Allbirds (I'm sustainably practical)

**The technique:** Ask the founder: "Describe the person who buys this. Not demographics — their values, their self-image, their aspirations." Mine THAT vocabulary. The name should mirror how the customer wants to be seen, not what the product does.

### Need-State / After-State Naming
Name the emotional or physical state the buyer wants to enter, escape, or restore. Adjacent to ritual mapping but more outcome-driven.

**Examples:** Calm (meditation app), Hero (cosmetics), Needed (supplements), Relief (CBD), Focus (nootropics), Glow Recipe (skincare), Bliss (spa)

**The technique:** Ask: "How does the customer feel BEFORE using this? How do they feel AFTER?" The gap between those two states is the naming territory. Mine the after-state vocabulary.

### Price-Signal Calibration
Deliberately tune the name's lexical register so it reads luxury, masstige, clinical, natural, or value BEFORE the shopper reads any copy. The name silently sets price expectations.

| Register | Characteristics | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Luxury** | French/Latin roots, restraint, white space implied | Aesop, Le Labo, Byredo |
| **Masstige** (mass + prestige) | Clean, modern, slightly elevated | Glossier, Drunk Elephant, Olaplex |
| **Clinical/Scientific** | Compound names, technical suffixes, abbreviations | CeraVe, SkinCeuticals, NIOD |
| **Natural/Clean** | Anglo-Saxon roots, earth words, simplicity | Seed, Grove, Branch Basics |
| **Value-by-design** | Deliberately plain, anti-luxury positioning | The Ordinary, Brandless, No Name |

**The test:** Show the name with no other context. Ask: "What price do you expect to pay?" If the answer doesn't match the actual price point, the name is miscalibrated.

### Front-of-Pack Hierarchy Fit
Test the name inside the full packaging stack — not in isolation. Consumer names almost never appear alone on a package.

**The test:** Write out the full label text:
```
[BRAND NAME]
[Product descriptor]
[Benefit claim]
[Scent/Flavor/Variant]
[Size/Volume]
```

Does the brand name hold its weight when stacked above "Daily Resurfacing Body Serum — Bergamot & Ylang Ylang — 200ml"? Some names are strong alone but collapse under descriptor weight. Short, punchy names (Aesop, Lush, Glow) hold up. Long compounds can compete with the descriptor for attention.

### Thumbnail / Commerce-Tile Testing
Many first impressions now happen in 80-pixel boxes on phones, not on shelves. Test names in mobile commerce contexts.

**The test:**
- Mock up the name in an Amazon product tile (tiny, cropped, compressed)
- Mock it in an Instacart grid next to competitors
- Mock it in a TikTok Shop listing
- Mock it in an email subject line: "Your [Brand Name] order has shipped"

Short names with distinctive letter shapes win in thumbnails. Long names or names with many similar letters (minimum, millennium) become illegible blurs.

### Badgeability / Wearability
The best consumer brand names don't just identify — they DISPLAY. Would a customer proudly show this name on a tote bag, water bottle, hoodie, or hat?

**The test:** "Would you wear a hat that says [name] on it?" If yes → high badgeability. If you'd need to explain it → medium. If you'd be embarrassed → low.

**High badgeability:** Supreme, Yeti, Patagonia, Le Labo, Aesop, Liquid Death
**Low badgeability:** Names that are too generic (Fresh, Clean), too long (Beautycounter), or too confusing out of context

### Adjacent-Category Code Borrowing
Borrow naming codes from a more desirable neighboring category to reposition the brand. This premiumizes fast and moves the product into a new mental aisle.

| Your category | Borrow from | Effect | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| Skincare | Fragrance | Elevates from clinical to sensorial | Le Labo, Byredo (now skincare too) |
| Non-alcoholic drinks | Spirits | Signals sophistication, ritual | Seedlip, Monday, Ritual Zero |
| Supplements | Fashion/beauty | Signals lifestyle, not medicine | Hims, Hers, Needed, Ritual |
| Home goods | Hospitality/hotel | Signals elevated experience | Casper, Brooklinen, Parachute |
| Pet food | Human food/restaurant | Signals quality, premium | The Farmer's Dog, Ollie, Nom Nom |

**The technique:** Ask: "What adjacent category do you WISH your product was associated with?" Mine that category's naming conventions.

### Subculture Code-Switch Naming
Mine the vocabulary, cadence, references, and tonal rules of a specific scene. The name should feel fluent to insiders without becoming unreadable to everyone else.

| Subculture | Vocabulary style | Example brands |
|---|---|---|
| **Sneaker culture** | Coded, alphanumeric, model-number energy | Palace, Brain Dead, A-COLD-WALL |
| **Natural wine** | Irreverent, hand-drawn, anti-pretension | Gut Oggau, Meinklang, Gulp Hablo |
| **Wellness internet** | Soft, aspirational, Sanskrit-adjacent | Moon Juice, Sakara, Erewhon |
| **Skate** | Blunt, confrontational, absurdist | Fucking Awesome, Anti Hero, Toy Machine |
| **Gaming** | Technical, abbreviated, dark | Razer, HyperX, Corsair |

**The technique:** Identify the target subculture. Study 10-15 brands that are credible within that scene. Extract the naming PATTERN (not individual names). Generate candidates that follow the pattern without copying any specific brand.

**Anti-pattern:** Don't borrow subculture codes you don't belong to. A corporate wellness brand using skate naming will be immediately detected and rejected by the target audience.

### Trend-Cycle Audit
Separate names that are culturally current from names that are merely trend-dependent. Will this name still work in 5 years?

**Current trend-dependent naming patterns (2024-2026):**
- "The [Noun]" pattern (The Ordinary, The Outset, The Nue Co) — currently saturated
- Single abstract adjectives (Needed, Actual, Real) — peaking
- Deliberately ugly/confrontational names (Liquid Death, Ugly, Crud) — spreading to mainstream, may age
- Clinical compound names (CeraVe, SkinCeuticals) — steady, less trend-dependent
- Place-evocative names (Patagonia, Brooklyn, Williamsburg) — enduring
- Artisanal/craft-coded names (hand-drawn energy, lo-fi, anti-corporate) — currently strong in natural wine, craft food, indie beauty. Durable aesthetic but the naming convention may mainstream and lose its edge.
- Wellness-ethereal names (Moon Juice, Sakara, adaptogen-adjacent) — peaking, may age as wellness culture evolves
- DTC-minimal names (Hims, Hers, Curology, Ro) — clean and direct but the pattern is saturating
- One-syllable found words (Grit, Hush, Loom, Bolt) — timeless structural pattern, unlikely to date

**The test:** "Would this name have worked in 2015? Will it work in 2030?" If yes to both, it's trend-resistant. If only current, it may date fast.

### Semantic Polysemy (Deliberate Double Reading)
Design names that mean different things to different audiences. The dual reading creates insider appeal while maintaining broad accessibility.

**Examples:**
- **Crush** — physical destruction (Gen Z energy) AND romantic affection (broader audience)
- **Bloom** — heat bloom on the tongue (foodie insider) AND flowers blooming (general warmth)
- **Culture** — bacterial culture (maker insider) AND good taste/sophistication (general)
- **Proof** — alcohol proof/tested strength (spirits insider) AND evidence/verification (general)

**The technique:** For each finalist name, ask: "What does this word mean to a 22-year-old? A 45-year-old? A subject-matter expert? A casual browser?" If it means something interesting to each, the polysemy is productive.

### Relational / Cousin Naming
Product family names that share DNA with the masterbrand without being identical. Creates brand unity on shelf without predictable compound patterns.

**Examples:**
- Bumble → Bizz, BFF (shared B-sound, truncated energy)
- Glossier → Glossier Play, Boy Brow, Cloud Paint (all evoke the core "gloss" sensibility without repeating it)
- Apple → iPhone, iPad, iMac (shared prefix system)

**The technique:** Before finalizing a brand name, ask: "When we launch 3 more products, what do the sub-names sound like?" If you can generate 3 natural-feeling cousin names, the masterbrand has good family potential.

### Memetic / Viral Engineering
Names specifically designed to be shareable, quotable, or meme-ready. The name becomes content, not just identification.

**Why some names go viral:**
- **Shock/dissonance:** Liquid Death, Fucking Awesome, Who Gives A Crap — the name IS the share-trigger
- **Catchphrase potential:** "I need my [name]" / "Have you tried [name]?" / "[name] changed my life" — the name fits naturally into social captions
- **Screenshot-worthy:** The name looks good in a text message, DM, or tweet without additional context

**The test (adult brands):** "Would someone screenshot this name and send it to a friend with no caption?" If yes, the name has memetic potential.

**The test (kids/family brands):** The viral mechanic is different for family products. Test: (a) "Would a child demand this by name in a store?" (b) "Would a parent film a 'cute moment' involving this brand and share it?" (c) "Would a parent text another parent 'you need to get [name] for your kid'?" The viral loop for kids' brands runs through the child's enthusiasm → parent's sharing instinct, not through adult screenshot-DM behavior.

### Co-Created / Community Naming
Using the brand's community to propose or vote on naming elements. Increases buy-in and makes consumers feel like co-owners.

**When to use:** Flavor naming, limited editions, seasonal drops, new product lines within an established brand. Less appropriate for the masterbrand itself (too high-stakes for crowdsourcing).

**Examples:** Ben & Jerry's flavor naming captures fan energy. Lego Ideas invites community product design. Many craft breweries let taproom regulars name special releases.

### Consumer Portfolio Grammar
Build naming RULES for the full product system: flavors, scents, shades, limited editions, seasonal drops, collaborations, bundles, and hero products.

**Example systems:**

| Brand | Grammar | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Le Labo** | [Ingredient] + [Number] | Santal 33, Rose 31, Bergamote 22 |
| **Glossier** | Evocative single words | Cloud Paint, Boy Brow, Futuredew |
| **Nike** | Model + Colorway + Collab | Air Max 1 "Patta" Monarch |
| **Ben & Jerry's** | Pun/cultural reference | Half Baked, Cherry Garcia, Phish Food |
| **The Ordinary** | Clinical ingredient name | Niacinamide 10% + Zinc 1% |

**The technique:** Before finalizing the masterbrand, define the naming grammar for at least: (a) product variants/SKUs, (b) limited editions, (c) collaborations. If the grammar doesn't flow naturally from the masterbrand, the system will break at scale.

### Story-World / Lore-Seeding
Choose names that can spawn a larger universe — campaigns, characters, rituals, seasonal chapters, editorial voice, collaborations, in-jokes. This is forward-looking myth-making, not backward-looking founder narrative.

**Examples:**
- **Liquid Death** → "Murder Your Thirst," the Liquid Death Country Club, coffin cases, the entire death-metal-water universe
- **Drunk Elephant** → the "suspicious 6" ingredients to avoid, the marula fruit origin myth, the "smoothie" mixing ritual
- **Aesop** → the literary/fable connection enables endless storytelling without ever explaining products

**The test:** "Can I imagine three marketing campaigns, two collaborations, and a limited edition that all feel native to this name?" If yes, the name has lore potential. If it only supports literal product description, it doesn't.

### Distinctive-Asset Seeding
Favor names that naturally generate icons, mascots, monograms, symbols, or packaging motifs beyond the wordmark. Consumer recall is often visual before verbal.

**High asset-seeding potential:** Bumble (bee icon), Liquid Death (skull), Penguin (the penguin), Apple (the apple), Mailchimp (the chimp)
**Low asset-seeding potential:** Abstract coined names, generic adjectives, names with no concrete imagery

**The test:** "What's the obvious icon for this name?" If the answer comes in under 2 seconds, the name seeds assets naturally. If you have to think about it, the name will rely entirely on typography — which works (Aesop, Supreme) but limits the visual toolkit.

## Sound Symbolism Research

Research in sound symbolism (Köhler, Ramachandran, Sapir) shows consistent cross-cultural patterns:

### Vowel Associations
| Vowel Sound | Association | Example Names |
|---|---|---|
| ee, ih (front high) | Small, fast, light, precise, technical | Sprint, Fitbit, Zillow |
| eh, ah (front mid/low) | Open, warm, accessible, natural | Tesla, Alexa, Panda |
| oh, oo (back rounded) | Large, deep, powerful, luxurious | Google, Roku, Zoosk |
| uh (central) | Neutral, grounded, sturdy | Uber, Tumblr |

### Consonant Associations
| Consonant Type | Sounds | Association | Example Names |
|---|---|---|---|
| Plosives | b, d, g, k, p, t | Strong, decisive, energetic, impactful | Bolt, Kodak, Grab, TikTok |
| Fricatives | f, s, sh, v, z | Flowing, elegant, speed, sophistication | Figma, Sonos, Shein, Visa, Zoom |
| Nasals | m, n, ng | Warm, resonant, nurturing, communal | Mambo, Notion, Ning |
| Liquids | l, r | Smooth (l) or dynamic (r), organic | Loom, Lush, Roku, Rivian |
| Affricates | ch, j | Playful, energetic, youthful | Chime, Jet |
| Glides | w, y | Friendly, easy, approachable, conversational | Waymo, Yelp, Waze, Yahoo |

### Stress and Rhythm Patterns
- **Trochaic** (STRONG-weak): Feels natural in English. Most 2-syllable English words follow this. Examples: Apple, Google, Meta.
- **Iambic** (weak-STRONG): Feels more formal, aspirational. Examples: Chanel, DeLong.
- **Dactylic** (STRONG-weak-weak): Feels expansive, storytelling. Examples: Amazon, Palantir.

### Sound Progression
The sequence of sounds across syllables creates movement:
- **Hard → Soft** (Kindle, Bumble): Impact then resolution. Feels decisive but friendly.
- **Soft → Hard** (Slack, Stripe): Gentle approach then punch. Feels confident.
- **Consistent** (TikTok, Lulu): Repetition creates rhythm and memorability.

## Age-Gated Pronounceability (for products targeting children)

For products where children under 8 are a primary audience (kids' food, toys, children's media), apply stricter phonetic constraints than the standard phonesthetic framework:

| Constraint | Why | Examples that pass | Examples that fail |
|---|---|---|---|
| **2 syllables max** | Working memory for names is ~2 chunks in pre-literate children | Nook, Tumble, Nibble | Breviary, Colophon |
| **CVCV preferred** | Simplest syllable structure, universally pronounceable | Nunu, Bobo, Tama | Scratch, Plinth |
| **No consonant clusters** | Children under 5 struggle with /str/, /spr/, /bl/, /kr/ | Kiln, Berry, Panda | Stripe, Crux, Bramble |
| **Favor nasals + liquids** | /m/, /n/, /l/ are acquired earliest and feel warm | Murmur, Noodle, Lemon | Forge, Skarn |
| **Open vowels** | /a/, /o/ feel bigger, warmer, friendlier (bouba-class) | Tumble, Forage, Nook | Brisk, Grit, Pith |

**The test:** Ask a real or imagined 4-year-old to say the name. If they can't on the first try, the name has age-gated friction.

## Japanese Sound-Symbolism (Giseigo/Gitaigo)

Japanese has the richest sound-symbolic vocabulary of any major language — thousands of specialized words that describe actions, states, textures, and feelings through sound rather than meaning. These are not just onomatopoeia — they're a complete sensory vocabulary.

**CRITICAL: Read the Authenticity Calibration section (below) BEFORE generating.** Full reduplicative patterns (fuwa-fuwa, kira-kira) read as "trying to be Japanese" in English-language markets and are rejected by bilingual audiences. The real value is in half-forms as standalone words, CVCV phonotactic filtering, and real Japanese words with cross-cultural resonance. The authenticity section explains where the line is.

### The Three Categories

| Type | What it describes | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Giseigo** | Actual sounds | pika-pika (flash/sparkle), don-don (thunder/knock), zaa-zaa (heavy rain) |
| **Gitaigo** | States, conditions, textures | kira-kira (sparkling), fuwa-fuwa (fluffy/light), saku-saku (crisp/efficient), nuru-nuru (slimy/slippery) |
| **Gijougo** | Emotions, feelings | waku-waku (excited/anticipating), doki-doki (heart pounding), shiin (silence/stillness) |

### Gitaigo Organized by Sensory Domain

Use this as a generation vocabulary for consumer/lifestyle brands:

**Texture & Touch:**
| Word | Meaning | Brand energy |
|---|---|---|
| sara-sara | Smooth, flowing, silky | Clean, effortless, premium |
| zara-zara | Rough, coarse, textured | Raw, authentic, craft |
| fuwa-fuwa | Fluffy, soft, floating | Light, gentle, comfort |
| mochi-mochi | Chewy, springy, elastic | Playful, tactile, food-adjacent |
| sube-sube | Smooth, slippery (skin) | Skincare, beauty, polished |
| gasa-gasa | Dry, rough, rustling | Raw, natural, unfinished |

**Visual & Light:**
| Word | Meaning | Brand energy |
|---|---|---|
| kira-kira | Sparkling, glittering | Premium, jewelry, celebration |
| pika-pika | Flashing, shiny, new | Tech, clean, bright |
| gira-gira | Glaring, dazzling | Bold, intense, attention |
| bon-yari | Hazy, vague, soft-focus | Dreamy, atmospheric, ambient |

**Movement & Energy:**
| Word | Meaning | Brand energy |
|---|---|---|
| saku-saku | Crisp, efficient, crunching | Productivity, food, precision |
| sura-sura | Flowing smoothly, effortless | Ease, luxury, mastery |
| noro-noro | Slow, sluggish | Intentional slowness, slow fashion |
| kuru-kuru | Spinning, rotating | Dynamic, playful, cycle |
| suya-suya | Sleeping peacefully | Rest, calm, wellness |

**Emotional / Atmospheric:**
| Word | Meaning | Brand energy |
|---|---|---|
| waku-waku | Excited anticipation | Discovery, adventure, launch |
| shiin | Total silence, stillness | Meditation, focus, minimalism |
| hotto | Relieved, warm sigh | Comfort, home, safety |
| iki-iki | Lively, vital, fresh | Health, energy, renewal |

### How to Use in Naming

**Technique 1: Half-forms as standalone names.** Full reduplicatives (fuwa-fuwa, kira-kira) read as "trying to be Japanese" in English-language markets. Instead, take ONE element as a standalone word:
- kura (from kura-kura: "dizzy" — but also means "storehouse" as a standalone word)
- mochi (from mochi-mochi — but also the food, internationally known)
- saku (from saku-saku — also means "bloom" as a standalone)
- nuru (from nuru-nuru — unusual, distinctive, tactile)

**Technique 2: CVCV phonotactic filtering.** Japanese words are almost exclusively CVCV or CVN patterns (consonant-vowel-consonant-vowel). Use this as a structural constraint to generate globally pronounceable names. ANY CVCV name you generate will be natively pronounceable in Japanese, making it inherently bilingual.

**Technique 3: Real Japanese words with cross-cultural resonance.** Some Japanese words work as brand names in English markets because they carry meaning without requiring translation:

| Word | Meaning | Successfully used by | Why it works globally |
|---|---|---|---|
| **muji** | "no brand" / plain | MUJI | Radical simplicity as identity |
| **uniqlo** | "unique clothing" (portmanteau) | UNIQLO | Coined but sounds Japanese |
| **nuno** | cloth, fabric | — (available) | Material honesty, Filipino cognate |
| **kura** | storehouse | — (available) | Preservation, craft, heritage |
| **muro** | room, chamber | — (available) | Interior, contained, intimate |
| **washi** | Japanese paper | — (as brand name) | Craft, texture, tradition |
| **sumi** | ink, charcoal | — (available) | Dark, artistic, minimal |
| **tsugi** | next, joint, patch | — (available) | Continuation, mending (kintsugi adjacent) |
| **kawa** | river, leather (different kanji) | — (available) | Flow, material, nature |

### Authenticity vs "Weeb" — The Calibration

The line between "authentically Japanese-inflected" and "cringe appropriation" is real. Guidance:

**Reads as authentic:**
- Single real Japanese words with clean meaning (Muji, Kura, Nuno)
- CVCV names that feel Japanese without claiming to be (Velo, Moro, Saku)
- Words connected to genuine Japanese craft traditions (raku, washi, sumi)
- Understated, material, plain — wabi-sabi energy

**Reads as "weeb-ish":**
- Full reduplicatives used for non-Japanese products (calling a Brooklyn clothing brand "FuwaFuwa")
- Cherry-picking "cool" Japanese words with no product connection (calling a SaaS tool "Samurai")
- Mixing Japanese and English awkwardly ("ZenCraft," "WabiTech")
- Using Japanese script/characters as decoration without understanding

**The test:** Would a bilingual Japanese-English speaker in Shibuya find this name respectful or cringe? If you're not sure, it's probably cringe. When in doubt, use CVCV-structured names that SOUND Japanese-compatible without claiming Japanese origin.
