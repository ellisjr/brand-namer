## DEFINITIONS (Top 10 picks — obscure words and non-obvious meanings)

**Precept** (n.) — "a general rule intended to regulate behavior or thought"
  Etymology: Latin "praeceptum" (order, command), from "praecipere" (to instruct, warn)
  As a brand: "Every contract is measured against the precept — the standard of what should be"
  Secondary meaning: In law, a precept is a written command from a superior authority

**Assay** (n./v.) — "to test the quality of a metal or ore; to evaluate or examine"
  Etymology: Old French "essai" (trial, attempt), from Latin "exagium" (weighing)
  As a brand: "Every contract undergoes the assay — a test of quality and purity"
  Note: Shares etymology with "essay" and the French "essai"

**Attest** (v.) — "to affirm to be correct, true, or genuine; to certify formally"
  Etymology: Latin "attestari" (to bear witness to), from "ad-" + "testis" (witness)
  As a brand: "We attest to the quality and completeness of every review"

**Rubric** (n.) — "a set of instructions or rules; a scoring guide"
  Etymology: Latin "rubrica" (red earth/ochre) — medieval scribes wrote section headings in red ink
  As a brand: "The rubric defines the standard" — scoring frameworks for contract quality

**Probity** (n.) — "the quality of having strong moral principles; honesty and decency"
  Etymology: Latin "probitas" (uprightness, honesty), from "probus" (good, upright)
  As a brand: Signals institutional integrity without claiming legal status

**Essai** (n.) — "a trial, attempt, test; also an essay"
  Etymology: French, from Latin "exagium" (weighing). Same root as "assay" and "essay"
  As a brand: The test of every contract. ess.ai domain creates elegant recursion.

**Cupel** (n.) — "a small, shallow, porous cup used in assaying to separate precious metals from base metals"
  Etymology: French "coupelle" (small cup), from Latin "cupella"
  As a brand: "The cupel separates what's valuable from what's not" — perfect for contract analysis

**Percept** (n.) — "a mental impression of something perceived by the senses; the object of perception"
  Etymology: Latin "perceptum" (something perceived), from "percipere" (to seize, understand)
  As a brand: The act of perceiving clearly — what the platform enables

**Scrutin** (n.) — "a ballot, a formal vote; the act of examining closely"
  Etymology: French, from Latin "scrutinium" (examination, investigation)
  As a brand: Close examination of every clause. French sophistication.

**Ariston** — "the best, the most excellent"
  Etymology: Greek "aristos" (best, most excellent). Root of "aristocracy"
  As a brand: Excellence in legal analysis. Clean, international, 3 syllables.
