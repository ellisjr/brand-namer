## Definitions for Key Shortlisted Names

**Bramble** (n.) — a prickly scrambling shrub of the rose family, especially a blackberry bush. From Old English "brembel." The thorny exterior protecting sweet, dark fruit maps perfectly to bold heat protecting deep flavor.

**Brine** (n./v.) — water strongly saturated with salt; to preserve in salt solution. From Old English "bryne." The core medium of fermentation. One of the few words that IS the process.

**Nettle** (n./v.) — a wild plant with jagged leaves covered in stinging hairs; to irritate or provoke. From Old English "netele." Stings on contact but historically valued as medicine and food. The duality is the brand story.

**Crock** (n.) — an earthenware pot or jar, especially one used for fermentation or storage. From Old English "crocc." Also slang for something worthless — the irreverent double meaning adds character.

**Cultivar** (n.) — a plant variety that has been produced by selective breeding. From "cultivated variety." For a brand using locally-sourced peppers, the word captures both the agricultural craft and the cultural sophistication.

**Raku** (n.) — a type of Japanese pottery characterized by hand-shaping, rapid firing, and unpredictable glaze effects. From Japanese "raku" (enjoyment/ease). Each piece is unique — like small-batch sauce.

**Saltern** (n.) — a set of pools or vats in which seawater is evaporated to produce salt. From Old English "sealtern." The place where preservation begins.

**Koji** (n.) — a fungus (Aspergillus oryzae) used in East Asian fermentation to produce soy sauce, miso, and sake. The engine of umami. Increasingly recognized in Western craft food circles.

**Sorrel** (n.) — a wild plant with arrow-shaped leaves and a sharp, acidic taste, used in cooking. Also: a reddish-brown color. From French "surelle" (sour). The color matches aged hot sauce; the flavor matches the tang.

**Wort** (n.) — an infusion of malt or other grain before fermentation; also Old English suffix meaning "plant/herb" (as in "liverwort," "mugwort"). From Old English "wyrt." In brewing, the liquid you ferment. In herbalism, any healing plant.
