### Naming Territories Defined (5 territories)

**Territory 1: Terroir & Earth**
- Emotional promise: Rooted, place-specific, honest craft
- Semantic field: soil, clay, loam, valley, sediment, root, strata, basin, till, humus
- Phonetic profile: Rounded vowels, nasal consonants (m, n), grounded weight
- Anti-patterns: NOT precious/pretentious, NOT farm-to-table cliche

**Territory 2: Ferment & Alchemy**
- Emotional promise: Transformation, patience, slow magic, process mastery
- Semantic field: culture, brine, bloom, steep, cure, char, flux, tincture, mash, still
- Phonetic profile: Liquid consonants (l, r), fricatives for flow
- Anti-patterns: NOT scientific/sterile, NOT "artisanal brewery" generic

**Territory 3: Heat & Sensation (Non-literal)**
- Emotional promise: Bold sensory experience without cliche fire/burn language
- Semantic field: scorch, sear, ember, smolder, welt, sting, prickle, flush, sweat, singe
- Phonetic profile: Plosives (k, t, p) for punch, short syllables
- Anti-patterns: NOT "fire," "flame," "burn," "inferno"

**Territory 4: Creature & Nature (Wild, not cute)**
- Emotional promise: Untamed, specific, imageable, slightly dangerous
- Semantic field: wasp, viper, crow, hornet, thorn, briar, thistle, nettle, feral
- Phonetic profile: Hard consonants, angular, sharp
- Anti-patterns: NOT cute animals, NOT Yellowbird/Secret Aardvark territory

**Territory 5: Irreverent Found Word**
- Emotional promise: Unexpected, memorable, "wait, what?" -- Fly By Jing / Liquid Death territory
- Semantic field: Arbitrary associations, verbs-as-brands, colloquial, slang
- Phonetic profile: Short (1-2 syllables), punchy
- Anti-patterns: NOT trying too hard, NOT faux-edgy

### Definitions for Top Picks

**Brine** (n.) — Water saturated with salt, used for preserving food.
Etymology: Old English "bryne" (salt water).
As brand: Captures the core process while sounding sharp and appetizing.

**Loam** (n.) — Rich, fertile soil composed of sand, silt, and clay.
Etymology: Old English "lam" (clay, earth).
As brand: The earth the peppers grow in. Terroir in a word.

**Peat** (n.) — Partially decomposed organic matter found in bogs.
Etymology: Medieval Latin "peta."
As brand: Smoky, earthy, aged -- mirrors fermentation. Evokes peat-smoked whisky.

**Marl** (n.) — Crumbly mixture of clay and calcium carbonate used to fertilize soil.
Etymology: Old French "marle."
As brand: The soil beneath the farms. Obscure enough to own, grounded enough to feel authentic.

**Fermata** (n.) — A musical notation indicating a pause or hold.
Etymology: Italian "fermata" (a stop), from "fermare" (to stop).
As brand: Fermentation = patience = the hold. Contains "ferment" within it.

**Dank** (adj.) — Damp and cold; moldy. (Slang: excellent, high-quality.)
Etymology: Middle English, of Scandinavian origin.
As brand: Dual reading for two audiences. Fermentation IS dank (literal). Good sauce IS dank (slang).
