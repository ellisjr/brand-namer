## The Three Pillars of a Great Name

David Placek of Lexicon Branding (who named BlackBerry, Swiffer, Sonos, Azure, Vercel, Windsurf, Impossible Foods) identifies three pillars for effective brand names:

### 1. Build for Trust
The name must feel credible and reliable. In an era of institutional skepticism, the name is often the first trust signal. Names that feel invented-for-marketing (Synergix, Optimacore) trigger skepticism. Names that feel grounded — either in real language or in pleasing phonetics — earn initial trust.

### 2. Create Interest
The name must generate curiosity. "The most popular names in your marketing department are usually the most comfortable — aka boring." Great names involve some ambiguity. They make you lean in rather than nod and move on. If your team is immediately comfortable with a name, you probably don't have the right one yet.

### 3. Deliver Meaning
The name should carry or suggest meaning — but not over-explain. The best names operate on multiple levels: a surface meaning that's immediately graspable, and deeper layers that reveal themselves over time. "Swiffer" sounds like the action of using the product. "Sonos" suggests sound operating as a system. "Impossible" challenges you to reconsider what's possible.

## Emotional Dimensions of Names

Names don't just denote — they connote. Every name occupies a position in emotional space. Jennifer Aaker's Brand Personality Framework identifies five core dimensions, each of which maps to naming strategies:

### The Five Personality Dimensions (Aaker, 1997)

**Sincerity** (honest, wholesome, cheerful, down-to-earth)
- Names that feel: warm, approachable, familiar, grounded
- Phonetic tendency: soft consonants (m, l, n), open vowels, 1-2 syllables
- Examples: Honest Company, Warby Parker, Mailchimp, Etsy

**Excitement** (daring, spirited, imaginative, up-to-date)
- Names that feel: energetic, bold, surprising, kinetic
- Phonetic tendency: plosives (k, p, t, b), sharp front vowels, punchy rhythm, staccato syllables
- Examples: TikTok, Snapchat, Bolt, Zoom, Riot Games

**Competence** (reliable, intelligent, successful, leader-like)
- Names that feel: precise, authoritative, institutional, trustworthy
- Phonetic tendency: hard consonants for authority, clean syllables, balanced stress, high/front vowels (i, e) for precision and tech-forward feel
- Examples: Palantir, Vanguard, Bloomberg, Stripe, Intel

**Sophistication** (upper class, charming, elegant)
- Names that feel: refined, luxurious, exclusive, cultured
- Phonetic tendency: fricatives (s, v, f), back vowels (oh, oo), flowing rhythm, longer syllables
- Examples: Chanel, Aston Martin, Hermès, Rivian

**Ruggedness** (outdoorsy, tough, strong)
- Names that feel: bold, physical, enduring, raw
- Phonetic tendency: hard consonants (g, k, d), short syllables, Anglo-Saxon roots, nasal sounds (m, n) for grounded warmth
- Examples: Patagonia, Yeti, Caterpillar, Wolverine

### Quick Reference: Phoneme → Personality Mapping
| Phonetic Quality | Maps To | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Soft consonants (m, l, n) + rounded vowels | Sincerity | Feels warm, human, approachable |
| Plosives (k, p, t) + sharp front vowels | Excitement | Feels explosive, kinetic, attention-grabbing |
| High/front vowels (i, e) + clean consonants | Competence | Feels precise, tech-forward, intelligent |
| Fricatives (s, v, f) + back vowels (oh, oo) | Sophistication | Feels flowing, elegant, luxurious |
| Hard consonants (g, k, d) + short syllables | Ruggedness | Feels tough, grounded, physical |
| Nasals (m, n, ng) | Calm, human, nurturing | Feels resonant and embodied |
| Glides (w, y) | Sincerity, Regular Guy | Feels friendly, easy, conversational |

### Using this in naming:
During Loop 1 (Brand Brief, 1a), identify which personality dimension(s) the brand targets. During Loop 2 (Generation, 2a), use the phonetic tendencies as generation constraints. During Loop 3 (Shortlisting, 3a), evaluate candidates against the target personality.

### The Three Layers of Name Signaling
Brand names signal personality through three layers working together. Treat these as separate but combinable levers:
1. **Semantics** — what the word literally or metaphorically suggests
2. **Phonetics** — how it sounds in the mouth and ear
3. **Structure** — length, rhythm, stress, novelty, and word shape

Sound beats meaning in early exposure when the name is coined or ambiguous. Meaning beats sound when the semantic cue is very strong. Structure (syllable count, stress pattern, word shape) quietly influences both.

### Granular Vowel Perception Map
| Vowel | Perception | Useful For | Example Feel |
|---|---|---|---|
| **i, ee** | Small, bright, quick, precise, youthful | Tech, agility, cleanliness | Intel, Visa, Nike |
| **e** | Clear, intelligent, modern, lean | Competence, clarity, approachability | Tesla, Lego |
| **a** | Broad, open, bold, human | Scale, warmth, confidence | Amazon, Canon |
| **o** | Rounded, stable, premium, complete | Trust, sophistication, calm strength | Rolex, Google |
| **u, oo** | Deep, soft, plush or heavy | Comfort, luxury, durability | Uber, Roku |

### Phonetic Recipes by Perception Goal
| Goal | Sound Recipe | Semantic Direction |
|---|---|---|
| Trustworthy & warm | Softer consonants, nasals, rounded vowels | home, care, honest, whole, natural |
| Fast & innovative | Short form, plosives, high/front vowels, x/z/k accents | spark, shift, snap, vector, pulse |
| Premium & elegant | Liquids + fricatives + rounded vowels | maison, reserve, aura, atelier, velvet |
| Tough & durable | Hard stops, back vowels, blunt stress | forge, ridge, stone, field, timber |
| Intelligent & institutional | Disciplined structure, moderate novelty, crisp consonants | oracle, index, vantage, summit, accord |

### Novelty Level Spectrum
Where a name sits on this spectrum determines its tradeoffs:
- **Descriptive** (General Electric, PayPal) → clearest, easiest, least ownable
- **Suggestive** (Slack, Stripe, Kindle) → best balance for most startups
- **Arbitrary** (Apple, Amazon, Blackberry) → memorable with good branding support
- **Coined** (Xerox, Kodak, Häagen-Dazs) → strongest ownability, highest explanation burden

**Caution:** Premium and trustworthy are not the same. Premium often wants polish; trust often wants familiarity. A name for a serious B2B product often works best in the Competence + Ruler or Competence + Sage zone, not pure Excitement.

| **Innocent** | Safety, simplicity | Pure, simple, clean words | soft, open, light | purity, goodness, nature | Dove, Honest, Aveeno |
| **Explorer** | Freedom, discovery | Directional, geographic, expansive | broad, directional, mobile | frontier, path, horizon | Patagonia, North Face, Safari |
| **Sage** | Truth, wisdom | Concise, intellectual, exact | crisp, exact, restrained | truth, insight, knowledge | Oracle, Google, Intel |
| **Hero** | Mastery, courage | Strong, decisive, upward | punchy, forceful | victory, endurance, defense | Nike, Duracell, FedEx |
| **Outlaw** | Liberation, rebellion | Sharp, disruptive, confrontational | jagged, dark, disruptive | rebellion, taboo, breakage | Virgin, Diesel, Vice |
| **Magician** | Transformation | Mystical, soft-to-hard shifts, luminous | fluid, luminous | transformation, wonder | Disney, Polaroid, Dyson |

| **Lover** | Intimacy, pleasure | Sensual vowels, lyrical flow | sensual, flowing | beauty, intimacy, indulgence | Godiva, Venus, Chanel |
| **Jester** | Enjoyment, play | Bouncy, repetitive, surprising | bouncy, repetitive | fun, surprise, delight | Skittles, Yahoo, M&M's |

|---|---|---|---|

| **Outlaw** | Sound-Symbolic, Contrarian, Found Word | Coined | Institutional Compound, Descriptive |
| **Magician** | Coined, Evocative, Foreign-Root | Metaphorical, Blends | Descriptive, Acronymic |
| **Regular Guy** | Found Word, Descriptive, Verb-as-Brand | Compound | Foreign-Root, Classical Stems |
| **Lover** | Evocative, Foreign-Root | Sound-Symbolic, Blends | Institutional Compound, Acronymic |
| **Jester** | Sound-Symbolic, Blends, Found Word | Compound | Foreign-Root, Institutional |
| **Caregiver** | Evocative, Personified, Found Word | Descriptive | Sound-Symbolic (hard), Contrarian |
| **Creator** | Coined, Compound, Metaphorical | Found Word, Tech Branding | Descriptive |
| **Ruler** | Institutional Compound, Foreign-Root, Found Word | Classical Stems, Evocative | Sound-Symbolic (playful), Blends |

### Using archetypes in naming:
Ask the user which archetype resonates (or infer from the brief). Use the archetype to guide both the naming category and the emotional territory. Cross-reference with the matrix above — a "Sage" brand should gravitate toward Found Word, Foreign-Root, or Institutional Compound categories. A "Jester" brand should explore Sound-Symbolic and Blends.

### Prototype targeting (Rosch)
For any archetype or personality dimension, there's a *prototypical* name — the name that feels maximally "right" for that personality. Identifying the prototype — even if it's taken — gives you a concrete target to generate toward. "The most [Sage] name I can imagine is Oracle. I can't use Oracle. What's available that's equally Sage?" This is more actionable than abstract personality descriptors because it gives you a specific sound, length, and feel to aim at. Ask the user: "What existing brand name — from any industry — best captures the energy you want?" That name becomes the prototype target.

## How Names Build Meaning Over Time

Every brand name exists on a spectrum from "arrives full of meaning" to "arrives empty and fills up."

### Names That Arrive with Meaning
These names carry built-in semantic content. The advantage: instant comprehension. The risk: they can feel limiting as the brand evolves.

- **Amazon** — Arrived meaning: vast river, biodiversity, scale. Bezos explicitly wanted a name that conveyed "biggest."
- **Patagonia** — Arrived meaning: remote, pristine, adventurous. The place name carries the brand ethos.
- **Impossible Foods** — Arrived meaning: challenge, ambition, defiance of expectations.
- **Salesforce** — Arrived meaning: power + sales. Immediately communicates the category.

### Names That Arrive Empty (Blank Canvas)
These names have little or no inherent meaning. The advantage: infinite flexibility. The risk: requires more brand-building investment to create associations.

- **Google** — Started as a math term (googol) most people didn't know. Now means "to search."
- **Xerox** — Derived from Greek "dry" but nobody knew that. It became synonymous with photocopying.
- **Häagen-Dazs** — Completely invented. Means nothing in any language. Evoked European craftsmanship through phonetic suggestion alone.
- **Kodak** — George Eastman invented it because he liked the letter K. Pure phonetic construction.

### The Hybrid Sweet Spot
The most effective names often occupy a middle ground: they suggest something without over-explaining it.

- **Slack** — Real English word, but recontextualized. Suggests "taking up the slack" but doesn't describe what the product does.
- **Stripe** — A real word that suggests clean lines, simplicity, directness. Doesn't say "payments."
- **Kindle** — Means "to ignite." Suggests sparking imagination, starting a fire (of reading). Doesn't say "e-reader."
- **Swiffer** — Invented, but sounds like the motion of using it. Phonetically light and fast.

### Strategic Implication:
The "right" position on this spectrum depends on the brand's competitive context. In a crowded, noisy category, a more meaningful name helps cut through. In a new category where you're defining the space, a blank-canvas name gives you room to grow. Ask the user during Loop 1 (1a Brand Brief) which situation they're in.

### Availability Heuristic (Tversky & Kahneman, 1973)
People judge importance and likelihood by how easily examples come to mind. For naming: if a name instantly triggers a vivid association (good or bad), it has high "availability" — it will be easy to recall but is also anchored to that association. Names that trigger no associations have low availability — harder to remember but a true blank canvas. This maps directly to the spectrum above. High-availability names (Amazon, Caterpillar) arrive loaded with imagery. Low-availability names (Xerox, Kodak) require brand investment but accumulate uncontested meaning. During evaluation, ask: "When you hear this name, what's the first thing that comes to mind? If the answer is 'nothing' — is that a feature or a bug for this brand?"

## Cognitive Processing: Why Some Names Stick

Research in cognitive psychology reveals why some names are effortlessly remembered while others vanish:

### Processing Fluency (Alter & Oppenheimer, 2009)
Names that are easy to process are perceived as more trustworthy, more familiar, and more likable — even on first encounter. This is called the "fluency heuristic."

Fluency drivers in names:
- **Pronounceability**: Can you say it without hesitation?
- **Spelling predictability**: Can you spell it from hearing it?
- **Phonological simplicity**: Fewer consonant clusters, natural stress patterns
- **Length**: Shorter names are processed faster (2-3 syllables is the sweet spot)

Caveat: Fluency builds trust, but distinctiveness builds memory. The best names balance both — they're easy to process BUT don't sound like everything else.

### The Distinctiveness Advantage (Von Restorff Effect)
Items that stand out from their context are remembered better. In naming: a name that breaks the pattern of its competitive set gets a memory advantage.

- **Apple** in a sea of IBM, DEC, HP → the organic word stands out
- **Impossible** in a sea of Beyond, Boca, Morningstar → the adjective stands out
- **Mailchimp** in a sea of Constant Contact, Campaign Monitor, SendGrid → the animal stands out

### Mere Exposure Effect (Zajonc, 1968)
People develop preferences for things simply because they've been exposed to them repeatedly. This means: even a name that feels "weird" initially will come to feel natural with enough exposure. Lexicon's Placek confirms this — clients almost never have an instant "aha" moment. The name that initially felt uncomfortable is often the one that performs best.

### Concreteness Advantage (Paivio, 1991)
Concrete, imageable words are remembered better than abstract ones because they activate both verbal and visual memory systems ("dual coding"). Names that evoke a mental image have a built-in memory advantage.

- **Concrete/Imageable**: Snowflake, Caterpillar, Dropbox, Shell → instant mental picture
- **Abstract**: Accenture, Cognizant, Synopsys → no mental picture, harder to remember

### Cognitive Chunking (Miller, 1956)
Working memory holds ~7±2 "chunks." Names that can be processed as a single chunk (1-2 syllables, familiar phoneme patterns like "Stripe" or "Slack") consume minimal cognitive resources. Names that require multiple chunks (unfamiliar consonant clusters, 4+ syllables, ambiguous stress) tax working memory and feel harder to "hold onto." During evaluation, ask: how many chunks does this name require? A familiar compound like "Benchmark" is two easy chunks. An invented word like "Cognivex" is three uncertain chunks. Fewer chunks = lower cognitive load = easier recall.

### Semantic Satiation Test
Say a candidate name aloud 50 times in a row. If it still feels meaningful and connected to its intended associations after repetition, the meaning is robust — it's deeply encoded. If it dissolves into meaningless sound, the semantic content is fragile and depends on novelty. Found words and metaphorical names tend to resist satiation better than coined words, because their meaning is grounded in existing knowledge. Use this as a gut-check on finalists in 4e (Stress Tests).

### Primacy and Recency Effects (Serial Position)
People remember the first and last items in a list better than items in the middle. In naming sessions:
- **Presentation order matters.** The first name in a batch and the last name get disproportionate attention. Rotate the order across batches to prevent positional bias.
- **In batch-of-10 presentations:** Names at positions 1, 2, 9, 10 get a memory advantage. Names at positions 4-7 get forgotten. If a name consistently gets picked from mid-list positions, it's genuinely strong — it overcame the positional disadvantage.
- **In final presentations:** Put your strongest recommendation first (primacy) or last (recency), not in the middle.

### Choice Overload (Iyengar & Lepper, 2000)
When presented with too many options, people make worse decisions or avoid deciding entirely. The famous "jam study" showed 24 options produced fewer purchases than 6.

In naming sessions:
- **Galleries of 100+ names can paralyze.** The batch-of-10 presentation format mitigates this — it constrains each decision to a manageable set.
- **Final shortlists should be 5-9 names, not 15-20.** More than 9 finalists triggers choice overload.
- **Tier the final presentation.** Tier 1 (3-5 names, clean), Tier 2 (2-4 names, caveats), Tier 3 (reference only). The user's primary decision space is Tier 1.
- **If the user can't decide between finalists,** reduce the set: "Let's eliminate one at a time. Which name would you cut first?" Subtraction is psychologically easier than selection.

## The Lexicon Insight: Letters Have Personalities

Lexicon Branding's proprietary research (confirmed across multiple studies) shows that individual letters carry consistent psychological associations:

| Letter | Association | Example Names |
|---|---|---|
| **V** | Most alive, vibrant, energetic | Corvette, Viagra, Vercel, Visa |
| **B** | Most reliable, trustworthy | BlackBerry, BMW, Bosch |
| **Z** | Attention-grabbing, electric | Azure, Zoom, Zara, Zillow |
| **X** | Innovation, forward-thinking | Xerox, Xbox, SpaceX |
| **K** | Strong, decisive, impactful | Kodak, Kindle, Nike (hard K sound) |
| **S** | Smooth, flowing, sophisticated | Sonos, Samsung, Stripe |

Additionally:
- **Double letters** signal more features and capabilities ("Zepp" feels more feature-rich than "Zep")
- The letter **i** feels more innovative than **y** (Flickr → innovative; Flicky → less so)
- **Palindromes** feel balanced and complete (Sonos, Navan)

## The Feeling Spectrum: Mapping Name Emotions

Names operate across multiple emotional dimensions simultaneously. Use these axes to evaluate candidates:

### Authority ←→ Approachability
- Authority: Bloomberg, Palantir, Vanguard (formal, institutional, commanding)
- Approachability: Slack, Notion, Bumble (casual, friendly, welcoming)
- Question: Does the brand need to be respected or liked first?

### Innovation ←→ Heritage
- Innovation: Vercel, Tesla, Neuralink (future-facing, cutting-edge)
- Heritage: Brooks Brothers, Patek Philippe, Rolls-Royce (established, time-tested)
- Question: Does the brand's value come from what's new or what endures?

### Precision ←→ Playfulness
- Precision: Stripe, Clearbit, Databricks (exact, technical, no-nonsense)
- Playfulness: Mailchimp, Snapchat, Woot (fun, irreverent, lighthearted)
- Question: Does the brand promise accuracy or delight?

### Intimacy ←→ Scale
- Intimacy: Bumble, Warby Parker, Casper (personal, close, warm)
- Scale: Amazon, Salesforce, Oracle (vast, powerful, enterprise)
- Question: Does the brand feel like a friend or a force?

### Simplicity ←→ Complexity
- Simplicity: Slack, Bench, Calm (clean, clear, uncluttered)
- Complexity: Palantir, Anthropic, DeepMind (layered, intellectual, deep)
- Question: Does the brand's value come from removing complexity or mastering it?

### Using the spectrum:
During Loop 1 (1a-1b), ask where the brand should sit on 2-3 of these axes. During shortlisting (3a), evaluate whether each candidate name actually lands where intended. A name that's trying to be "approachable" but sounds "authoritative" has a positioning mismatch.

## Open Door vs Closed Door Names

A name is not just a label — it's a narrative frame. Professional namers evaluate names by the story they invite people to tell.

### Closed-Door Names
Tell you exactly what the product is. Little imaginative space.

- Examples: FileSync Pro, AI Code Navigator, BugFix Assistant, Cloud Backup Manager
- **Strengths**: Immediate comprehension, lower launch friction, strong for SEO/category clarity
- **Weaknesses**: Hard to stretch into broader story, easy to outgrow, difficult to premiumize, sound generic
- A closed-door name says: "You already understand me. There is no second layer."

### Open-Door Names
Suggest a world rather than a function. Create room for meaning to accumulate over time.

- Examples: Stripe, Figma, Notion, Linear, Slack, Pharos, Luma
- **Strengths**: Brandable over time, invites curiosity, supports product expansion, enables community identity and brand mythology
- **Weaknesses**: Requires more brand-building investment to establish initial comprehension
- An open-door name says: "There is something here to discover."

### The Balanced Sweet Spot
The strongest names often suggest without over-explaining — enough meaning to orient, enough openness to grow.

### Story Potential Questions
For each candidate, ask:
- Does the name imply a **world**, not just a tool?
- Can the company tell multiple future stories with it?
- Does it create **curiosity without confusion**?
- Does it leave room for product expansion?
- Can customers project identity into it?

### Story Types a Name Can Carry
- **Guide story** (Pharos, Beacon, Northstar) — trust, direction, competence
- **Maker story** (Forge, Craft, Foundry) — agency, skill, builders
- **Clarity story** (Lucent, Luma, Clearbit) — insight, simplification, intelligence
- **Motion story** (Linear, Drift, Vector, Stride, Surge, Arc) — speed, progress, flow. Motion names suggest the user is going somewhere and the product accelerates the journey. They work especially well for tools that help users navigate complexity, reach decisions faster, or transform inputs into outputs. Strong sub-types: forward motion (Stride, Advance, Propel), directional (Vector, Bearing, Course), convergence (Converge, Nexus, Confluence), and transformation (Shift, Pivot, Flux, Phase). See the Motion & Direction semantic field in naming-categories.md for full generation guidance.
- **Mythic story** (Nike, Atlas, Oracle) — stature, symbolism, larger-than-function meaning

### Name Evaluation Label
For each candidate, classify:

| Label | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Closed / strong | Descriptive but effective for the category |
| Closed / fragile | Descriptive and likely to be outgrown |
| Balanced | Suggests without over-explaining |
| Open / strong | Evocative with clear emotional direction |
| Open / too vague | So abstract it creates confusion, not curiosity |

## Naming Failure Patterns

A naming tool should warn on these seven failure categories. Based on real naming disasters:

### 1. Global Linguistic Collision
Slang, profanity, insults, or taboo meanings in target languages.
- *Mitsubishi Pajero* — derogatory in Spanish, requiring rebranding to "Montero" in those markets.

### 2. Context Volatility
External events redefine a name's associations.
- *Isis mobile wallet* → renamed to Softcard after the extremist group dominated headlines. The name itself wasn't bad; the world changed around it.
- **Warning**: Screen not just dictionaries but current news associations.

### 3. Ridicule Shape
Clunky compression, fake-tech endings, acronym residue, overdesigned corporate neologisms.
- *Tribune Publishing → tronc* — reverse-engineered from "Tribune online content." Mocked immediately, reverted within two years.
- **Warning**: If a name feels reverse-engineered from an internal phrase, ridicule risk is high.

### 4. Patronizing Worldview
Gendered, classed, infantilizing, or exclusionary framing.
- *BIC for Her* — implied ordinary pens were for men. Triggered widespread mockery.
- **Warning**: Demographic labels attached to generic products signal condescension.

### 5. Strategic Mismatch / Heritage Destruction
Replacing trusted legacy meaning with abstraction.
- *Royal Mail → Consignia* — consulting jargon replacing a culturally loaded national institution. Reverted within one year.
- *Kraft → Mondelez* — coined name felt committee-designed. Russian speakers noted obscene phonetic resemblance.
- **Warning**: High risk when replacing a culturally loaded legacy name with a generic corporate abstraction.

### 6. Over-Explanation / Low Narrative Ceiling
Name is too literal to scale beyond its initial product scope.
- **Warning**: If the name can only describe what the product does today, it will constrain tomorrow.

### 7. Urban Legend False Alarms
Not every cross-language issue is real. The Chevy Nova "no va" story is widely debunked (Snopes).
- **Warning**: Require evidence before issuing severe cross-language warnings. Distinguish real risks from folklore.

## Rapid Evaluation Checklist: SMILE and SCRATCH

Adapted from Alexandra Watkins' (Eat My Words) framework. Use as a quick-pass filter during 3a (Shortlisting) — run every candidate through both lists in under 30 seconds.

### SMILE (what a good name does)
| Letter | Quality | Quick test |
|---|---|---|
| **S** | Suggestive | Does it hint at something relevant without over-explaining? |
| **M** | Memorable | Will someone remember it after hearing it once? |
| **I** | Imagery | Does it evoke a mental picture or sensory experience? |
| **L** | Legs | Can it extend into taglines, campaigns, sub-brands, and future products? |
| **E** | Emotional | Does it trigger a feeling — warmth, curiosity, confidence, excitement? |

A name that scores 4-5 on SMILE is a strong candidate. A name that scores 1-2 needs significant brand investment to overcome its deficiencies.

### SCRATCH (what a bad name does)
| Letter | Problem | Quick test |
|---|---|---|
| **S** | Spelling challenged | Would someone misspell it after hearing it? |
| **C** | Copycat | Does it sound like a competitor or follow an exhausted pattern? |
| **R** | Restrictive | Does it box you into one product, geography, or audience? |
| **A** | Annoying | Is it forced, try-hard, or cringe-inducing? |
| **T** | Tame | Is it so safe that it's forgettable? |
| **C** | Curse of knowledge | Does it only make sense to insiders? |
| **H** | Hard to pronounce | Will people hesitate or mangle it? |

Any hit on SCRATCH is a yellow flag. Two or more hits is a strong signal to deprioritize unless the name's SMILE scores are exceptional.

### Using SMILE/SCRATCH in the pipeline:
- **3a (Shortlisting):** Fast-pass every candidate. Deprioritize high-SCRATCH names, advance high-SMILE names.
- **4e (Stress Tests / Finalist evaluation):** Run formally on the top 3-5. Include scores in `03-validation.md` and reference in the final presentation rationale.
- **During generation:** Use SMILE as a generation prompt: "Generate names that are high on Imagery and Emotional." Use SCRATCH as a self-check: "Am I generating Tame names? Copycat names?"
