# Elicitation Techniques for Brand Naming

Read this file when you need to deepen ideation, stress-test candidates, or unstick a session. These techniques are adapted from structured creative methodologies (BMAD Method's Creative Intelligence Suite, de Bono's Six Thinking Hats, and classical brainstorming frameworks) and reframed specifically for brand naming.

Not every technique is needed every time. Use them when:
- Phase 2 generation feels too safe or convergent → SCAMPER, Constraint Removal
- Phase 5 shortlisting needs rigor → Six Thinking Hats, Stakeholder Role-Play
- Phase 8 variation generation is producing weak derivatives → SCAMPER on existing candidates
- The user is stuck or unhappy with all options → Inversion, Constraint Removal
- The shortlist needs stress-testing before final presentation → Pre-Mortem Analysis

---

## 1. SCAMPER for Naming

SCAMPER is a structured creativity framework that forces systematic variation. Apply it to individual name candidates OR to the naming brief itself.

### Applied to a name candidate (e.g., "Vantage"):

| SCAMPER Lens | Question | Example Output |
|---|---|---|
| **Substitute** | Replace a syllable, phoneme, or morpheme | Vantix, Vantaro, Vintage |
| **Combine** | Merge with another candidate or concept | VantagePoint, VantaFlow |
| **Adapt** | Borrow from another language or domain | Avantaj (French-inflected), Vanta (shortened, like Vantablack) |
| **Modify** | Change stress, length, or spelling | Van'tage (apostrophe), VNTG (vowel-stripped), Vantáge (accent) |
| **Put to other uses** | Reframe what the word evokes | "Vantage" as a viewing position → name for an analytics company instead |
| **Eliminate** | Strip to the essential sound | Van, Tage, Vant |
| **Reverse / Rearrange** | Flip order, read backward, anagram | Egatnav → discard. TageVan → interesting? Navant → has potential |

### Applied to the naming brief itself:
- **Substitute** the audience: "What if this product were for teenagers instead of enterprise buyers? What name would work?"
- **Combine** two brand personality traits: "What names are both 'clinical' AND 'playful'?"
- **Eliminate** a constraint: "What if the name didn't need to hint at the product category at all?"

### When to use:
- Phase 2 when a naming category is producing bland results — run SCAMPER on the best candidate from that category
- Phase 8 (Variation Generation) as a systematic alternative to the prefix/suffix approach
- Anytime the user likes a name's "direction" but not the name itself

## 2. Inversion / Reverse Brainstorming

Instead of asking "what's a good name?", ask "how would we guarantee this is a terrible name?" Then invert the findings.

### The process:
1. **Generate anti-names:** "Give me 10 names that would absolutely tank this brand. Names that are confusing, forgettable, offensive, or impossible to spell."
2. **Analyze why they fail:** Extract the specific failure modes — too generic, sounds like a competitor, unpronounceable consonant clusters, negative connotations, etc.
3. **Invert each failure into a design principle:** "Unpronounceable" → the good name must pass the "phone test" (can you say it clearly over a bad connection?). "Sounds like a competitor" → the good name must be phonetically distinct from the top 5 competitors.
4. **Apply the inverted principles** as a filter on existing candidates or as generation constraints for a new round.

### Example:
- Anti-name: "Synergix" → fails because it's a generic tech portmanteau that could be any company
- Inverted principle: the name must be specific enough that hearing it alone gives you a hint about what makes this company different
- Applied: filter out any candidates that could plausibly be the name of 3+ other types of companies

### When to use:
- When the user can articulate what they DON'T like but can't say what they DO like
- When early generation rounds are all producing similar-feeling names
- As a sharpening exercise before convergent shortlisting (Phase 5)

## 3. Pre-Mortem Analysis

A pre-mortem assumes the brand name has already been chosen and has caused problems. It surfaces risks that optimism-bias hides during the excitement of picking a name.

### The process:
1. For each finalist name, imagine it's 18 months post-launch.
2. Generate 3-5 failure scenarios:
   - "Customers keep confusing us with [other company]"
   - "The name became an unintentional joke / meme"
   - "We couldn't expand internationally because the name means [X] in [language]"
   - "People consistently misspell it when searching, costing us SEO traffic"
   - "The name pigeonholed us and now we can't expand into adjacent markets"
   - "A trademark dispute forced a rebrand"
3. Assess each scenario's likelihood (low / medium / high)
4. For any medium/high risks, decide: can this be mitigated, or should the name be downgraded?

### When to use:
- After Phase 10 (Trademark Screening), before final presentation
- When the user is emotionally attached to a name but you've spotted potential issues
- As a final gut-check: "Before I present these as the final shortlist, let me stress-test them"

## 4. Six Thinking Hats for Name Evaluation

Evaluate each finalist name from six distinct perspectives. This prevents the common trap of evaluating names only on "do I like how it sounds?" and ensures all dimensions are considered.

### The six perspectives applied to naming:

**White Hat (Facts & Data):**
- How many syllables? What's the stress pattern?
- Is the .com available? What about social handles?
- Are there trademark conflicts?
- What does web search reveal about existing usage?

**Red Hat (Emotions & Gut Feel):**
- What's your instant emotional reaction to hearing this name?
- Does it feel warm or cold? Heavy or light? Premium or accessible?
- Would you feel proud saying "I work at [name]" at a dinner party?
- Does it spark curiosity or feel flat?

**Black Hat (Caution & Risk):**
- What could go wrong? (Cross-language issues, pronunciation problems, negative associations)
- Is there litigation risk?
- Could the name become dated? (Trendy suffixes like -ly or -ify may age poorly)
- Does it constrain future pivots or expansion?

**Yellow Hat (Benefits & Value):**
- What's the strongest thing about this name?
- How memorable is it compared to competitors?
- Does it tell a story or create an opening for storytelling?
- Would it look good on a billboard? A business card? An app icon?

**Green Hat (Creativity & Possibilities):**
- What unexpected brand narratives could grow from this name?
- Could it become a verb? ("Just Slack me" → "Just [your name] it")
- What visual identity directions does it suggest?
- What taglines naturally pair with it?

**Blue Hat (Process & Decision):**
- Across all five other hats, which names score strongest overall?
- Are there dealbreakers that override other positives?
- What additional information would change the decision?
- What's the decision framework: majority of hats positive = proceed?

### When to use:
- Phase 5 (Convergent Shortlisting) when the user is torn between multiple strong candidates
- Any time evaluation feels superficial or purely vibes-based
- When presenting to the user: organize your rationale by hat to show you've considered all angles

## 5. Constraint Removal

When every name is getting filtered out — domains taken, trademarks filed, sounds like a competitor — the session can feel stuck. Constraint Removal temporarily drops all practical barriers to find what the user actually wants, then works backward to something achievable.

### The process:
1. **Remove all constraints:** "Forget domains, trademarks, existing companies, and practicality. If you could name this anything — a made-up word, a common word, an emoji, a sentence — what would capture exactly the right feeling?"
2. **Let the user (or the AI) generate freely** under zero constraints. Often, this produces names that are more emotionally honest than the constrained versions.
3. **Identify the essence:** What makes the unconstrained favorites work? Is it the sound? The meaning? The length? The cultural reference?
4. **Re-introduce constraints one at a time** and generate new candidates that preserve the essence while satisfying each constraint.

### Example progression:
- Unconstrained favorite: "Lighthouse" (but .com is taken, and there are dozens of companies called Lighthouse)
- Essence: guidance, clarity, a beacon through complexity, nautical/coastal feeling
- Re-constrained variations: "Pharos" (the ancient lighthouse), "Beacon" (simpler), "Luminary" (same meaning family), "Tidemark" (nautical + precision), "Waylight" (compound, unique)

### When to use:
- When the user says "I don't like any of these" after multiple rounds
- When the validation phases have killed all the favorites
- As a reset mechanism when the session feels stuck or demoralized

## 6. Role-Playing / Stakeholder Perspectives

Evaluate names by adopting specific personas and experiencing the name through their lens. Different from Six Thinking Hats because it's about WHO is encountering the name, not HOW to think about it.

### Key personas to role-play:

**The First-Time Customer:**
- "I just saw this name on a Google ad. What do I think it does?"
- "Would I click on this, or scroll past?"
- "Can I remember this name 10 minutes later without writing it down?"

**The Word-of-Mouth Recommender:**
- "I'm telling a friend about this over coffee. Can I say the name without spelling it?"
- "Does the name give them enough to Google it and find it?"
- "Am I embarrassed or proud to recommend something called [name]?"

**The Investor / VC:**
- "I see this name on a pitch deck. Does it signal ambition or amateurism?"
- "Does the name suggest a venture-scale opportunity or a small lifestyle business?"
- "Is this name going to age well over a 7-10 year fund lifecycle?"

**The Journalist:**
- "I'm writing a headline: '[Name] Raises $10M to Transform [Industry].' Does it work?"
- "Is this name easy to type quickly on deadline?"
- "Does it lend itself to puns or wordplay in headlines? (This can be good or bad.)"

**The Recruiter / Future Employee:**
- "Would a senior engineer want '[Name]' on their resume?"
- "Does this name attract or repel the talent the company needs?"

**The International User:**
- "Can a Mandarin, Spanish, Hindi, or Arabic speaker pronounce this?"
- "Does this name carry any unintended connotations in major world languages?"

### When to use:
- Phase 5 when narrowing from 15 to 5 candidates
- When the user is in a specific industry where one persona matters much more than others (e.g., healthcare → the patient persona is critical)
- When the user hasn't considered how the name will function in real-world contexts beyond a screen

## 7. First Principles Thinking for Naming

Strip away every assumption about what "a name for X should sound like" and rebuild from ground truth. This technique fights the gravitational pull of category conventions — the reason every AI company sounds like every other AI company.

### The process:
1. **Identify the conventions:** "Names in this space tend to be [pattern]. Everyone uses [suffix]. The category assumes [tone]."
2. **Question each convention:** "Why do VC tools sound institutional? Is that actually required, or just inherited? What if we named this like a consumer brand? A place? A verb?"
3. **Rebuild from ground truth:** "What does this product actually DO for the user, stripped of all category language? If no naming conventions existed — if you were the first company in this space — what word would you paint on the door?"
4. **Generate from the rebuilt foundation:** Use the ground-truth description to seed a fresh generation round unconstrained by category norms.

### Example:
- Convention: "VC tools sound like Bloomberg, Palantir, Vanguard — institutional, classical, authoritative"
- Question: "Is authority actually what GPs need from their ops tool, or is it speed, clarity, and calm?"
- Ground truth: "This tool makes the chaos of 20 portfolio companies feel manageable"
- Fresh generation: Calm, Haven, Steadfast, Clearview, Helm — names that wouldn't emerge from "name an institutional VC platform"

### When to use:
- When all generated names feel like they belong to the same category template
- When the user says "these all sound like [competitor]"
- Early in Phase 2 as a category-breaking exercise before conventional generation
- During pool recovery (Phase 8) when validation killed conventional names and you need fresh territory

## 8. Red Team vs Blue Team

More adversarial than Pre-Mortem. Instead of imagining failure scenarios, actively construct the strongest possible case AGAINST each finalist name, then construct the strongest possible case FOR it. The collision between attack and defense reveals whether a name's strengths actually outweigh its weaknesses or just feel good.

### The process:
1. **Red Team (attack):** For each finalist, generate the harshest credible critique:
   - "This name will fail because..."
   - "A competitor could exploit this name by..."
   - "In 3 years, this name will embarrass you because..."
   - "The board will reject this because..."
   - Attack phonetics, semantics, domain availability, cultural risk, competitive positioning, and narrative ceiling
2. **Blue Team (defend):** For each Red Team attack, construct the strongest possible rebuttal:
   - "That's mitigated because..."
   - "Actually, that weakness is a feature because..."
   - "The alternative is worse because..."
3. **Score the exchange:** Which names survived the attack with their core value intact? Which crumbled? A name that's easy to attack AND easy to defend is mediocre. A name that's hard to attack is strong. A name that's easy to attack but has devastating defenses is interesting.

### When to use:
- Phase 5 when the user is torn between 2-3 strong finalists
- When the team needs to justify the name choice to skeptical stakeholders
- When Pre-Mortem feels too abstract and the user wants a more confrontational stress test
- After pool recovery, to pressure-test resurrected candidates

## 9. Analogical Reasoning for Naming

Find naming successes in parallel domains and extract the structural pattern that made them work, then apply that pattern to the current brief. This technique generates candidates that "feel right" without being obvious, because they're borrowing proven structures from outside the category.

### The process:
1. **Identify the brand's core quality** — not what it does, but what it *is* at the deepest level. "This is fundamentally a [translation/navigation/simplification/amplification] tool."
2. **Find parallel successes:** "What's the most admired brand name in [adjacent industry] that also does [core quality]?"
   - "What's the Stripe of healthcare?" (Stripe = clean, simple, precise → what's the healthcare equivalent of that energy?)
   - "What's the Notion of finance?" (Notion = flexible, personal, idea-shaped → what finance name carries that?)
   - "What's the Patagonia of developer tools?" (Patagonia = principled, rugged, aspirational → what dev tool name carries that?)
3. **Extract the naming pattern:** Why does the parallel name work? Is it the syllable count? The metaphor domain? The found-word simplicity? The phonetic texture?
4. **Apply the pattern to generate new candidates** in the current domain using the extracted structure.

### Example:
- Brief: Name an AI-powered legal research tool
- Core quality: "Makes the impenetrable accessible"
- Parallel success: Kindle (makes books accessible → ignites understanding)
- Pattern: Single-syllable verb with kinetic energy + metaphorical ignition
- Applied candidates: Spark, Parse, Sift, Distill, Forge

### When to use:
- Phase 2 when you need to break out of obvious category naming patterns
- When the user can articulate a brand they admire in another industry and wants "that energy"
- During creative riffing (Phase 6) to find unexpected directions from proven naming structures
- When conventional generation is producing names that all feel like they belong to the same competitive set

## 10. TRIZ for Naming (Contradiction Resolution)

TRIZ (Theory of Inventive Problem Solving) is an engineering framework for resolving contradictions without compromise. Applied to naming: when the brand brief contains competing requirements ("we need a name that's both X and Y, but X and Y seem incompatible"), TRIZ finds names that resolve the contradiction rather than splitting the difference.

### Common naming contradictions:
- **Institutional AND approachable** — BlackRock solves this: "Black" = authority, "Rock" = grounded/solid (not cold). Slack solves it differently: casual word, but backed by an enterprise product.
- **Technical AND human** — Anthropic solves this: Greek root (technical) + human-centered meaning. Stripe solves it: precise (technical feel) but a real English word (human).
- **Innovative AND trustworthy** — These pull in opposite directions: innovation = risk, trust = safety. Palantir solves it: mythological (signals deep knowledge/wisdom) + novel (no one else has it).
- **Global AND distinctive** — Generic enough to work everywhere vs. specific enough to be memorable. Zoom solves it: one syllable, universally pronounceable, but the Z-onset makes it distinctive.
- **Expansive AND precise** — Room to grow vs. clarity about what it does now.

### The process:
1. **Identify the contradiction:** "The brief asks for [X] AND [Y]. These seem to pull in opposite directions."
2. **Study how existing brands resolved the same contradiction.** Extract the structural move they made.
3. **Apply the resolution strategy** to generate new candidates:
   - **Separation in time:** Name signals one quality now, accumulates the other over time (Google: started technical, became friendly through use)
   - **Separation in structure:** One part of the name carries X, the other carries Y (BlackRock: "Black" = authority, "Rock" = grounded warmth)
   - **Separation in level:** The phonetics carry one quality while the semantics carry the other (Slack: sounds casual, means "take up the slack" = industrious)
   - **Transcendence:** Find a concept that is inherently BOTH X and Y without needing to split (Oracle: both authoritative AND wise, no contradiction)

### When to use:
- Phase 1 when the brand brief contains competing personality requirements
- Phase 5 when the user keeps rejecting names for being "too X" or "not enough Y"
- When the shortlist keeps splitting into two camps and nothing bridges them
- As a targeted generation technique when the user can articulate the specific tension they're trying to resolve

## Combining Techniques

These techniques work well in sequence:

**For a stuck session:**
Constraint Removal → fresh unconstrained candidates → SCAMPER to create practical variants → Six Thinking Hats to evaluate

**For stress-testing a shortlist:**
Red Team vs Blue Team → Role-Play key stakeholders → Pre-Mortem on the top 3 → final decision

**For expanding a too-narrow pool:**
Inversion (identify what to avoid) → SCAMPER on the best existing candidates → Constraint Removal if still stuck → Analogical Reasoning to import patterns from parallel domains

**For breaking category conventions:**
First Principles (strip conventions) → Analogical Reasoning (import from adjacent domains) → SCAMPER to create practical variants of the best results

**For resolving competing requirements:**
TRIZ (identify the contradiction) → First Principles (question whether the contradiction is real) → generate candidates that resolve rather than compromise

**For a deep session with time:**
Run First Principles early (Phase 2) to establish ground truth → generate broadly → use Analogical Reasoning and TRIZ during riffing (Phase 6) → stress-test with Red Team/Blue Team and Pre-Mortem → final evaluation with Six Thinking Hats and Stakeholder Role-Play
